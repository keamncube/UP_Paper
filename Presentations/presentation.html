Regulation and bank lending in South Africa: A narrative approach

Dumakude Nxumalo, Keaoleboga Mncube, Xolani Sibande

University of Pretoria and South African Reserve Bank

2024-03-28

Introduction

  • Since the global financial crisis, regulatory changes have been a key focus for policymakers1.
  • However, there are concerns as to the impact of these changes on bank lending. This has been a focus of various studies.2
  • Given the importance of bank lending to economic growth, understanding the factors that influence bank lending is crucial.3

The aim of the study

  • Therefore, this study aims to investigate the impact of regulatory changes on bank lending in South Africa using a narrative approach.
  • We focus on two types of regulatory changes:
    • Macroprudential regulation
    • Finance and inclusion regulation
  • Estimate panel regression for six credit categories and four banks

Brief comments on literature

  • The literature on the impact of regulatory changes on bank lending is diverse.
  • Some studies find that regulatory changes have a negative impact on bank lending. Whilst other suggest that regulation does not impact bank lending.
  • In South Africa studies are limited, and therefore this question remains open.

Methodology

Narrative approach

  • The narrative approach requires construction of ‘exogenous’ narrative indices. Exogeneity is a key challenge in the literature.
  • The indices reflect regulatory events which the literature identifies as having a potential impact on bank lending.
  • However, the narrative approach is not without its limitations.
  • For example, the indices do not conform to any a priori expectations which can make interpretation challenging.
  • However, the narrative approach is useful in that it allows for the identification of regulatory events that may have been overlooked in the literature.

Construction of macroprudential indices

  • We delineate between anticipation and actual effect of the regulation
  • The anticipation effects is captured by the communication of macroprudential regulation,
  • Therefore, \(Draft_t\) take a value of 1 when there is communication and 0 otherwise.
  • Similarly, the actual impact if captured using the \(Implementation_t\) variable which take a value of 1 when macroprudential regulation is implemented, and 0 otherwise

Construction of finance indices

  • Following the same logic as the macroprudential indices, we construct two finance indices.
  • The first index captures proposed and final changes in finance regulation (\(FinReg_t\)), which take a value of 1 when such an event occurs, and 0 otherwise.
  • The second index captures the proposed national financial inclusion framework (\(FinInclusion_t\)), which takes a value of 1 when this event occurs, and 0 otherwise.

Construction of credit categories (segments)

  • Using the BA900, 930, and other data sources we constructed six credit categories.
  • Households: mortgages, unsecured credit, and secured credit
  • Corporate: mortgages, unsecured credit, and secured credit
  • Aggregates: total mortgages, total unsecured credit, total secured credit.

Econometric approach

  • We use a panel data approach to estimate the impact of regulatory changes on bank lending.
  • For each index the fixed effect model specified as follows: \[ Lending_{b,t}^{c} = \beta_1Index_t^i + \tau_b^c + \lambda_t^c + \beta'\Omega^c + \epsilon_{b,t}^c, and \] \[ Rate_{b,t}^{c} = \alpha_1Index_t^i + \tau_b^c + \lambda_t^c + \alpha'\Omega^c + \epsilon_{b,t}^c .\]
  • Where \(b\) is FNB, Absa, Nedbank, or Standard Bank. \(t\) is the time period, \(c\) is the credit category, and \(i\) constitutes the \(Draft_t\), \(Implementation_t\), \(FinReg_t\), or \(FinInclusion_t\) indices.

Econometric approach (continued)

  • Therefore \(Lending_{b,t}^c\) are the bank level three month growth of lending, and \(Rate_{b,t}^c\) are the bank level lending rates.
  • \(\Omega^c\) is a matrix of controls which includes a covid dummy, bank level variables, and other macroeconomic variables.
  • \(\tau_b^c\) captures the bank fixed effect, \(\lambda_t^c\) are the time fixed effects, and \(\epsilon_{b,t}^c\) are the error terms.

Discussion - Macroprudential regulation

  • The macroprudential regulation results suggests lending portfolio rebalancing phenomena. This result was also robust with controls.
  • In terms of the lending volumes results:
    • There is a rebalancing from long to short term lending.
    • Suggesting that banks are more risk averse toward term lending due to regulatory requirements

Discussion - Macroprudential regulation (continued)

  • In terms of the lending rates results:
    • Rebalancing means banks shift from unsecured credit to secured credit. This effect was not prevalent in mortgages.
    • The rebalancing effect was more pronounced in the household sector than in the corporate sector.
    • This is a similar risk averse behavior as in the lending volumes results.

Discussion - Finance and inclusion regulation

  • The finance and inclusion regulation results indicate a decrease in lending volumes and an increase in lending rates. However, the lending rates results are sensitive to controls.
  • Finance regulation:
    • The lending volumes specifically decrease in the unsecured lending for both corporates and households.
    • Mortgage lending volumes increase for households
    • For households and corporates lending rates increased for the secured credit, and an increase in lending rates for households mortgages.

Discussion - Finance and inclusion regulation (continued)

  • Finance inclusion:
    • Similar to finance regulation results but with stronger effects on lending volumes.
  • Overall and contrary to the intentions of the changes of these regulations, the results indicate a decrease in lending volumes and an increase in lending rates.

Conclusions

  • Macro prudential developments are working as intended. There is a portfolio rebalancing where banks move from riskier unsecured lending to the more prudent credit types
  • Finance regulations have the effect of restricting credit supply. In so far as financial inclusion is concerned, the actual effects are contrary to aims of fostering greater inclusion
